### Technical AML Questions (Your Answers)

**Q5: "Explain placement, layering, and integration."**

<details>
<summary>💡 Your Answer (Click to practice)</summary>

"Money laundering has three stages:

**Placement (Stage 1):** Getting illegal cash into the banking system. Criminal deposits $100k in cash, claims it's business revenue. Problem: Large deposits get flagged, so criminals split deposits, use multiple fronts, or lie about the source.

**Layering (Stage 2):** Moving the money through complex transactions to hide the source. This is your main focus. Money gets sent between accounts, countries, shell companies, etc. The goal: Create a confusing trail so investigators can't trace it back.

**Integration (Stage 3):** Making the money look clean. It becomes part of a business, gets 'invested' in real estate, or emerges as 'profit' from a fake company.

Your job in Transaction Monitoring: Catch money during Placement and especially Layering before it gets to Integration (which is hard to stop)."

**Time:** 2 minutes
**Energy:** Clear, structured, shows you understand the goal

</details>

**Q6: "Walk me through how you'd investigate a shell company."**

<details>
<summary>💡 Your Answer (Click to practice)</summary>

"I'd use the Golden Source Rule: verify independently, don't trust what company says.

Step 1: Search official business registry. Is the company actually registered?
Step 2: Check company financials (if available). Do they show real operations or are they empty?
Step 3: Analyze the director/ownership. Use People Search: Does the director have 47 other companies? (Red flag = professional nominee)
Step 4: Check OFAC/sanctions. Is anyone connected sanctioned?
Step 5: Call the company if possible. Do they actually answer? Can they describe their business?
Step 6: Look for patterns. Is this connected to other shells? Same address, same directors, same beneficial owners?

Red flags for shells: Virtual addresses, nominees, multiple companies by same person, no financial activity, impossible business models (1-month-old company with $100k transactions), offshore jurisdictions.

If I confirm it's a shell → Escalate immediately. Block transfers until verified. Request customer explanation."

**Time:** 2-3 minutes
**Energy:** Systematic, methodical, shows you know how to investigate

</details>

## Common "Trick" Questions & How to Handle Them

**Q: "What's a red flag that should NOT be escalated?"**

Answer: "A single red flag where there's obvious context. Example: An elderly customer's spending spikes in December (holidays). High spending + seasonal timing + retired = normal. No need to escalate. You escalate when multiple flags combine OR when a single flag has no explanation."

**Q: "You find the account meets 8 red flag criteria. What do you do?"**

Answer: "Escalate immediately for Enhanced Due Diligence. That many flags + that much severity = not my call alone. I'd prepare thorough documentation so my manager can make the decision quickly. If customer can't explain or provide documentation = recommend block/SAR filing. If they explain satisfactorily = proceed. But I'd escalate today."

**Q: "A customer is angry their transaction was blocked. How do you handle it?"**

Answer: "Empathetic but professional. I'd say: 'I understand this is frustrating. Our system flagged your transaction because [specific reason: new recipient, high amount, etc.]. We need documentation to verify before processing. Once you provide [specific docs], we can review quickly—usually within 24 hours.' I'd own the inconvenience without apologizing for the requirement. Compliance isn't optional."

**Q: "You disagree with your manager's decision on a case. What do you do?"**

Answer: "I'd respectfully raise my concern. I'd say: 'I understand your decision. I had flagged [X reason]. Can you help me understand your reasoning?' I'd listen to learn. If I still disagree after hearing them out, I'd say: 'I respect your call. Going forward, can we talk through cases like this together?' I'd trust the manager's judgment while advocating for my perspective."

## Energy & Presence Tips

### The 5-Minute Pre-Interview Ritual

Before you walk in:

1. **Breathwork (2 min):** 4-second inhale, 4-second hold, 6-second exhale. Do this 8 times. Calms your nervous system.

2. **Power Pose (1 min):** Stand tall, hands on hips or arms up. Sounds weird, but it actually boosts confidence hormones. Do it!

3. **Mental Visualization (1 min):** Imagine yourself answering a question confidently. See yourself smiling. See the interviewer nodding. See yourself getting the job offer.

4. **Positive Affirmation (1 min):** Say to yourself: "I've studied 15 days for this. I know AML. I'm good with people. I'm ready. They're lucky to have me in the room."

### During the Interview

**Body Language:**
- Sit up straight (not stiff, but engaged)
- Make eye contact
- Smile (genuine, not forced)
- Nod when they're talking (shows you're listening)
- Don't fidget or tap your leg

**Speech Patterns:**
- Speak clearly and at a normal pace (slow down if you rush)
- Minimize filler words ("um," "uh," "like")
- Pause for 2 seconds before answering (shows you're thinking, not just reacting)
- End statements with confidence (not as questions)

**Engagement:**
- Listen fully before answering
- Ask for clarification if unsure ("Can you clarify what you mean by...?")
- Show genuine curiosity about the role
- Ask good questions at the end (not "What's the salary?" yet—ask about team, growth, challenges)
