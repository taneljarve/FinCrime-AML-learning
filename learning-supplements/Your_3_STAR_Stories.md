## STORY 1: Attention to Detail / Pattern Recognition
**Best for:** "Tell me about a time you spotted something important others missed"

### The Story

**SITUATION:**
When I was trading futures, I developed a habit of analyzing volume patterns alongside price action. Most traders focused on trend lines and indicators, but I noticed that volume anomalies often preceded reversals that others didn't see coming.

**TASK:**
One morning on crude oil futures, I noticed the price was approaching a major support level. The technical setup looked like a continuation - textbook bullish reversal. But something felt off.

**ACTION:**
Instead of following the typical pattern, I pulled up the last 6 months of volume data at this support level. I compared the volume signatures - current day's volume was 40% lower than historical days when this level held. Lower volume at support = weaker conviction = higher probability of breakdown. I flagged this to my trading group. They disagreed - the charts looked bullish.

**RESULT:**
The market broke through support that afternoon. My analysis was right. The team started asking me to review volume patterns on key levels regularly. More importantly, I learned that data + pattern recognition beats following the crowd.

**WHY IT WORKS FOR FINCRIME:**
This shows pattern recognition (core AML skill), data-driven thinking, and having the courage to act on your analysis even when others disagree. In FinCrime, you'll spot red flags others miss - this story proves you can.

## STORY 2: Decision-Making Under Pressure / Incomplete Info
**Best for:** "Tell me about a time you had to make a difficult decision with incomplete information"

### The Story

**SITUATION:**
Trading means making decisions with imperfect information constantly. You never have 100% certainty. One day, I was watching emerging market currency futures and had to decide: Is this a short-term dip or the start of a major downtrend?

**TASK:**
I had about 70% of the information I wanted. The rest wasn't available yet. The position would require significant capital, and waiting meant potentially missing the move.

**ACTION:**
I built a quick framework in my head: (1) What do I know for certain? (2) What's my confidence level? (3) What's my downside if I'm wrong? (4) Do I have time to wait for more info? In this case, my confidence was 70%, my downside was manageable (stop loss in place), and waiting would cost the opportunity. I took the position.

**RESULT:**
I was wrong. The dip reversed and continued upward. I lost money. I learned something valuable though: sometimes imperfect decisions with good risk management beat perfect analysis that never happens. My framework was solid - the market just moved against me. I explained this to my mentor, and he said "Your process was good. Outcomes in trading aren't always in your control, but your decision-making is."

**WHY IT WORKS FOR FINCRIME:**
This shows you have a decision-making framework, you can handle being wrong, and you learn from losses. In FinCrime, you'll constantly make calls with incomplete info - this proves you can do it without freezing.

## STORY 3: Why Wise + Customer Focus
**Best for:** "Why do you want to work at Wise?" or "Tell me about a time you put customers first"

### The Story

**SITUATION:**
In my previous sales role, we had a major client who was pushing for a deal using aggressive tactics. There was huge pressure to close - our team needed the revenue. Half the team wanted to push hard and close; the other half wanted to slow down and build the relationship.

**TASK:**
We had one shot with this client. Internal disagreement was creating chaos. We needed to make a call, and we needed to be aligned.

**ACTION:**
Instead of picking a side, I suggested we talk to the customer directly. I asked: "What's actually keeping you from deciding?" It was a bold move in high-pressure moment. The customer admitted they didn't fully understand the product and felt rushed. They wanted to learn, not be sold to. I told the team: "Let's pivot. Let's educate, not sell."

**RESULT:**
We closed the deal - but more importantly, we realized customer-first approach worked better than aggressive tactics. The team adopted this approach after that. I got recognition for keeping us focused on what mattered: the customer's actual needs.

**WHY IT WORKS FOR FINCRIME:**
Two parts here. For "Why Wise": This connects to Wise's value "Customers > team > ego" - you believe in customer-first approach. For "Customer focus": You proved you prioritize customer needs over closing/selling. In FinCrime, protecting customers IS the job.
